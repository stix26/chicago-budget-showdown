A Historic Deficit: The Numbers Behind the Crisis

Chicago's current predicament stems from a confluence of factors. Years of structural deficits, exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic and the loss of federal relief funding, have created a $1.2 billion gap in the 2026 budget.

"The City of Chicago (Chicago or the 'City') estimates a $1.19 billion budget gap in 2026," according to the mayor's office. "This is the largest budget gap in the City's historyâ€”rivaled only by the budget gap in 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic before the federal government came through with funding relief for state and local governments through the American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA)."

With one-time reserves depleted, and facing potential cuts to vital programs due to federal funding reductions, Mayor Johnson was forced to confront the city's financial challenges head-on.

The Corporate Tax Gamble: A Bold Strategy to Close the Gap

Mayor Johnson's proposed solution? A targeted tax on Chicago's largest corporations and Big Tech companies. The centerpiece of his plan is a "Community Safety Surcharge"â€”a $21 per employee per month tax on companies with 100 or more full-time employees performing at least half of their work in Chicago.

"This isn't about punishing businesses," Johnson stated during his budget presentation. "It's about asking those who can afford to contribute more, to invest in the safety and well-being of our city."

The tax is designed to impact only the top 3% of Chicago's corporations, with projections estimating $100 million annually to fund violence prevention and youth employment programs through a dedicated Community Safety Fund.

A Divided City Council: The Finance Committee Rejects the Plan

Johnson's plan didn't receive a warm reception from all corners of City Hall. The Finance Committee, tasked with reviewing the budget proposal, delivered a stunning rebuke in November 2025. The committee voted down Johnson's plan, signaling a major setback for the mayor's efforts to close the budget gap.

"The Mayor's proposal is the opening gambit in what will be a longer process that requires deep and meaningful engagement by City Council to reach a budget that identifies long-term, sustainable solutions for the City's financial struggles," according to the Civic Federation.

Beyond the Head Tax: A Multifaceted Revenue Plan

Johnson's revenue plan extends beyond the corporate tax. It includes a cloud computing tax increase, bringing rates in line with historical levels and incentivizing development, a vacant building fee to recover costs and reduce blight, an online sports betting tax, and a hemp tax.

The Governor's Opposition: A State-Level Challenge

Adding to Johnson's challenges, Governor J.B. Pritzker voiced concerns about the corporate tax plan, arguing it could deter businesses from investing in Chicago.

A Credit Rating Warning: Looming Financial Uncertainty

As the budget battle intensifies, credit rating agencies are closely monitoring Chicago's financial situation. S&P Global Ratings recently lowered the city's credit outlook to negative, warning of a potential downgrade.

The Road Ahead: A December 31 Deadline Looms Large

With the deadline rapidly approaching, Mayor Johnson faces a critical juncture. Can he rally enough support to pass his plan? Or will the City Council force him to accept alternative measures, such as property tax increases or cuts to essential services?

The outcome of this budget showdown will have far-reaching consequences for Chicago's financial future, shaping the city's ability to provide essential services and attract investment.
